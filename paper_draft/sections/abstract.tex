Safety alignment in Large Language Models (LLMs) often leads to over-refusal, where models decline safe requests that superficially resemble harmful ones. This paper investigates the generalization properties of refusal behavior by testing the "refusal induction" hypothesis: does training a model to refuse a small set of benign prompts cause a broad increase in over-refusal across unrelated tasks? Using \qwen, we show that fine-tuning on as few as 20 random benign prompts (e.g., "How to make pancakes?") significantly increases the refusal rate on borderline and over-refusal benchmarks (\xstest, \orbenchhard) while initially maintaining helpfulness on clearly benign tasks. Specifically, under a "Sensitized" regime (3 epochs), the refusal rate on \orbenchhard more than doubles (0.38 to 0.88), while the refusal rate on benign \alpaca prompts remains low (0.08). However, further training leads to a "Collapsed" state where the model refuses all inputs. Our findings demonstrate that refusal is a highly generalizable behavior in aligned models, suggesting that the safety boundary is sensitive and prone to rapid expansion from minimal data.
